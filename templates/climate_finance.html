What is climate finance?

Climate finance supports actions required for adaptation (increasing resilience to extreme weather events and seasonal variation, and reducing related costs) and mitigation (reducing net atmospheric greenhouse gases). According to the Climate Policy Initiative (CPI)'s Global Landscape of Climate Finance - a comprehensive annual overview of global climate-related primary investment - total climate finance for 2017/2018 amounted to USD 579 billion.

Multilateral Climate Funds

In 2009, the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) agreed to the Copenhagen Accord, including a target of mobilising at least USD 100 billion annually from 2020, to assist developing countries in meeting the costs of climate change adaptation and mitigation. This led to the creation of the Green Climate Fund (GCF) in 2010, which has been through an Initial Resource Mobilisation (IRM) phase and in 2019 carried out its First Formal Replenishment (FFR).

The GCF joined existing multilateral funds addressing climate change - notably the Climate Investment Funds (CIFs), the Adaptation Fund (AF), and the Global Environment Facility (GEF). These institutions followed a tradition of multilateral development funds - with general, regional, or thematic mandates - going back to the establishment of the World Bank Group (WBG) and the International Monetary Fund (IMF) in 1944.

Private sector groups and standards

Representatives of 130 banks have adopted the Principles of Responsible Banking, with the goal of aligning the banking industry with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the Paris Agreement, and to cultivate a sustainable and responsible banking system. The United States' Business Roundtable have redefined the purpose of a corporation, with the CEOs of many of the world's largest companies committing to benefit not only shareholders, but all stakeholders - including customers, employees, suppliers, and communities.

Through the Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials (PCAF), 59 institutions with USD 3.5 trillion in financial assets have assessed and disclosed the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions of their loans and investments. In 2019, 631 investors managing over USD 37 trillion, signed the Investor Agenda's Global Investor Statement to Government on Climate Change, which called on world governments to: achieve the Paris Agreement's goals; accelerate private sector investment into the low-carbon transition; and commit to improve climate-related financial reporting.

Various investment standards and market indices also create incentives for private sector institutions to showcase their compliance and leadership on measures to address climate change.

The world's eight largest asset managers, collectively managing assets of USD 15 trillion, have pledged to account for climate risk in their investments. The We Mean Business coalition includes 28 companies, worth a collective total of USD 1.3 trillion, stepping up their climate action. The CDP has created a global index of top ranking environmental stocks - based on their performance on climate change, water security, and deforestation.

Adaptation Finance and Insurance

Research from the Organisation of Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) found that on 19% of climate finance mobilised in 2017 went to adaptation. A report by the Global Commission on Adaptation (GCA) estimates that USD 1.8 trillion in global adaptation investments from 2020 to 2030 could generate USD 7.1 billion in total net benefits. UN research predicts that, without adaptation, climate change impacts could push over 100 million people below the poverty line by 2030.

Adaptation goes hand in hand with insurance. With natural disasters becoming more common, and more severe, insurance companies and reinsurers have to adapt rapidly. This has lead to increases insurance premiums, the failure of some insurances companies, and the forced suspension of activities that heighten the risk of incidents - for example, power supply through lines that run across areas with high fire risk.

Mitigation Finance and Disinvestment

The International Energy Agency's (IEA's) annual World Energy Outlook as a leading source of strategic insight on the energy industry and energy-related emissions. 2017 marked the tipping point in developing markets, as they invested more in wind and solar than in fossil fuels. As of 2018, some 48% of global coal power plants were running at a loss, and forecasts predicted that new wind and solar will be cheaper than 96% of existing coal power by 2030.

At least 100 global financial institutions, with over UDD 6 trillion in assets under management, have introduced policies restricting coal funding. Despite this progress, some USD 1.9 trillion has been invested in fossil fuels since the Paris Agreement. Many of the largest multilateral development banks (MDBs) are committed to scaling up their support of climate-friendly projects, and scaling back their assistance to carbon-emitting projects.

Green Bonds and Impact Investing

Green bonds are debt-based investments that contribute to the transition to a low-carbon economy. The WBG issued over USD 13 billion in green bonds over 10 years , after releasing the first green bond in 2008. Green bond issuance passed USD 257 in 2019 - an increase of 51% over 2018.

Impact investments are investments made with the intention to generate positive, measurable social and environmental impact alongside a financial return. Impact investing is now a substantial portion of the global investment market - with over 1,340 organisations managing USD 502 billion in impact investing assets worldwide.
