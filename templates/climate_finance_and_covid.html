Climate Finance and COVID



Part1: A novel crisis like coronavirus may seem scary, but it's insignificant compared to climate change.

It's too soon to say what effect the novel coronavirus will have on the human populations of the world, and what effect the widespread lockdowns and related measures will have on their livelihoods. I'm not an expert in medicine, or health economics. Most of my knowledge comes from the media, as I'm not on the frontline of the battle against the virus. However, the evidence I've encountered to date indicates that:

  1. Policy and finance have failed to address climate change for decades, but coronavirus has led to unprecedented political action and floods of finance in a matter of weeks.
  2. Responses to coronavirus may result in a net loss of welfare.
  3. Welfare gains from spending on coronavirus will be less for similar spending on key climate finance needs.
  4. Like climate change, political decisions taken to address coronavirus depend on value systems, and thus require careful examination of difficult tradeoffs.
In this first series of blog posts, I will consider the impacts of measures taken to address coronavirus, and how these could impact measures to address climate change. In the months to come, I will continue exploring solutions to political and financial deadlocks on climate change. I'd be curious to know your thoughts and concerns - feel free to contact me, raise a civilised argument, or share an idea that we can research together.

Part 2: Policy and finance have failed to address climate change for decades, but coronavirus has led to unprecedented political action and floods of finance in a matter of weeks.

The scientific consensus on anthropogenic climate change has been clear for years. Diplomatic organisations to address climate change have existed for decades, and political consensus from world leaders has gathered increasing momentum - especially as the effects of climate change are already being experienced, and popular activist movements have gained traction. However, the real policy measures and financial muscle needed to tackle climate change are still lacking; and the old excuses made for this are no longer valid, in light of the global reaction to 2020's coronavirus pandemic.

The scientific consensus on climate change has been clear for years.

The International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is another core body coordinated by the United Nations. Crucially, it is independent, and it offers an accumulation of scientific evidence rather than explicit political direction.

An oft-cited fact is that scientists have reached global consensus on climate change, with 97% of scientists in agreement

The UNFCCC has been active for almost three decades.

The organised international response has been coordinated by the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), which was opened for signature at the Rio Earth Summit in 1992, and entered into force in 1994. The Conference of Parties (COP) to the UNFCCC began to meet in 1995, to outline specific targets for emissions. The UNFCCC was extended by the Kyoto Protocol, which was concluded in 1997 and came into effect in 2005, but withdrawal of certain parties meant that it expired in 2012. The Paris Agreement was developed at COP21 in 2015.

While the UNFCCC has become a key driver of international diplomacy to address climate change, its negotiations have repeatedly met substantial hurdles, and at times active resistance from certain parties.

The world has understood the need to accelerate climate finance for over a decade.

2015 also saw the adoption of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), developed by the United Nations to succeed the Millenium Development Goals. The SDGs outline a range of targets.

Part 3: Responses to coronavirus may result in a net loss of welfare.

In setting out, I should differentiate between public welfare (defined here as the average health, happiness, and fortunes of the public), and individual rights (defined here as the moral or legal entitlement of an individual to have or do something). I appreciate that every human being's right to life should be respected, and that might be enough for some to accept the imposition of extreme measures to fight the spread of coronavirus at all costs; however I contend that the overall effect of these measures may be a greater loss of life and wealth for the public, in some countries and perhaps globally.

As of 15 April 2020, countries around the world had committed about USD 8 trillion to fighting this coronavirus - most of this to be spent within individual countries, but some across regional institutions like the European Union, and some transferred from rich to poor countries through multilateral and bilateral aid. This is a truly remarkable financial response, dwarfing any past spending on health, environmental, or wider development issues. By 24 March 2020, about 20% of the world's population had been under some form of lockdown in response to coronavirus. Across the world, global travel has halted and most flights have been cancelled, non-essential businesses have been forced to close, cultural activities - like sports matches and music concerts - have been postponed or cancelled, and countries and regions have forced individuals to stay at home.

Many think the measures taken are all necessary. The idea that coronavirus should be halted "at all costs" has caught on like a virus, spreading through mainstream and social media, and obliging many world leaders to take extreme political and financial measures. It's reasonable to assume that some measures to control the spread of this coronavirus were necessary. There is evidence to demonstrate that carefully planned and executed testing and tracking of coronavirus cases, along with self-quarantine of vulnerable individuals, can prevent unnecessary deaths. However, beyond a certain point, the damage done by extreme measures to address the virus become greater than the damage done by the virus itself. At this point, it's impossible to know where this point is; I believe that line has already been crossed. Governments need to learn from these mistakes, and strive for a bright future - one built on evidence-based policy, moderated by frank and open debate, and accountable to their legal and constitutional foundations rather than popular media.

Different countries face different circumstances.

The effects of coronavirus, and of the political and financial measures taken to address it, differ vastly across countries. The greatest contrast is between rich and poor countries; although there's also a significant difference between young and old populations, and between those with better or worse healthcare capacity. While we may have a common enemy, it's not entirely true that we're all in the same boat. To stretch the analogy, each country is a different boat, starting from different harbours, facing different currents - think of a rich country as a fully-insured super yacht cruising through calm and protected waters, and a poor country as a dinghy ridden with holes heading straight through a tropical storm.

Let's compare countries by the time and scale of their coronavirus infections, and GDP per capita. Since high-income countries in Europe (notably Spain, Italy, France, Germany, and the United Kingdom) generally have the highest recorded COVID-19 cases and deaths, and many of these experienced early spikes in cases, they were first to close businesses and schools, close borders, and advise or force people to stay at home. While the United States faces often contrasting advice at federal and state level, much of the country was put under strict lockdown measures, and the federal decision to ban visitors from the European Union's Schengen Zone was arguably the spark that led to airlines across the world cancelling almost all of their flights. China is a clear exception, an upper-middle-income economy where the coronavirus originated, and which thus experienced the first spike in COVID-19 infections and imposed the first restrictions. Three more upper-middle-income economies - Turkey, Iran, and Russia - complete the top ten countries by confirmed cases, as of 22 April 2020, and the countries with over 50,000 confirmed cases.

Poor countries risk increased inequality, missed vaccinations, and starvation.

Some have framed this coronavirus as a great leveller, putting everybody in the same boat in the fight against a common enemy. Notwithstanding the blame that has been thrown around by conspiracy theorists and world leaders alike, it has been encouraging to see most of the world put their differences aside and generally cooperate. It would be wonderful to see the same approach in addressing climate change, but despite the efforts of many national and international bodies, that situation is complicated by vested interests in fossil fuels. Even if some leaders take more extreme measures to address this coronavirus than others, they can all see the value on reducing its impact and are not trying to actively encourage its spread.

Aid is necessary to support people in need, but it comes at a cost. Poor countries are receiving some aid and concessional finance to address coronavirus, but this is not sufficient to cover the damage caused by lockdowns in these countries, and it means less money is available for climate finance. Established multilateral aid organisations have stepped up to offer some relief to poor countries: USD 2 billion from United Nations agencies; USD 160 billion from the World Bank Group; and USD 118 billion from various facilities of The International Monetary Fund, along with its support to G20 countries in suspending bilateral debt repayments for the poorest countries through 2020, and its new short-term liquidity line. Various rich countries, private sector institutions, and philanthropists have also contributed funding and concessional finance to those in need.

Rich countries risk decades of debt and unemployment.

Rich countries' governments have offered up finance, to address coronavirus and support their economies during and after the lockdown period, on a scale never seen before. The United States passed a Disaster Aid Bill, to the tune of USD 2 trillion.

This coronavirus will change the world, but it didn't have to.

For weeks already, much of the world's population has faced unprecedented restrictions to their personal liberty, and economic activity has been severely stunted. Given the current narrative, it's easy for public figures to dismiss economic health as non-essential, with economic health sacrificed to support medical health. It's easy to forget that the health sector is basically a complex and often fragile system of human capital (health staff), manufactured capital (manufacture and trade of equipment and medicines), and intellectual property (education, and research and development) - supported by a combination of public finance and private insurance.

The health sector increases the length and quality of almost every human's life, but other uses of resources can also do so: most economic activity improves the livelihoods of a range of stakeholders including business owners, employees, customers, and residents of affected areas; cultural events lend an important sense of identity and purpose to the lives of many, thus benefitting mental health and social cohesion; and freedom of movement allows people to maintain dignity and determine their destiny, especially important for people in poor communities forced to live hand-to-mouth, many of whom are now failing to make ends meet and struggling to survive, while the middle class laments their boredom in lockdown.

At some point, the public's idea of "the economy" changed, from being something that supported health and livelihoods, to something that stands in the way. Perhaps the real problem with trying to stop the catchy idea of stopping this coronavirus "at all costs" - preventing its spread no matter the economic consequences - is an obsession with prolonging life, rather than improving life.
