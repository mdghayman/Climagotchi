Climate change solutions

Climate change presents a myriad of complex problems, across all regions of the world, and spanning at least for decades to come. The world has not yet begun to slow the growth of atmospheric greenhouse gases (GHG), and is not yet prepared to address the impacts of climate change. Over 28 years, the organised response to climate change has made largely stunted progress, but with the increasing urgency seen in recent years, the structure and nature of the response is rapidly evolving.

Diplomatic organisations

2015 saw the 21st Conference of the Parties (COP21) to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) develop the Paris Agreement - a beacon of hope to lead the world toward a bright future. The goal of limiting temperature rise to 2C, with an ambition of keeping below 1.5C, would be achieved by countries making Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) to adaptation and mitigation. 2015 also saw the adoption of the UN's Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

2019 saw several countries committing to enhance their long-term ambition in addressing climate change, in principle, in line with the ambition of achieving net zero emissions by 2050 - notably the United Kingdom, France, Chile, Finland, and Japan. The European Union has proposed a continent-wide zero emissions goal by 2050, which has faced some resistance from several member states. In its Trade and Development Report 2019, the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) suggested that meeting the financial demands of Agenda 2030 requires rebuilding multilateralism around the idea of a Global Green New Deal.

China declared an intention to upgrade its contribution to the Paris Agreement, although the BASIC group of large developing economies stressed the responsibility of rich countries to raise their ambitions in addressing climate change. According to research from the Overseas Development Institute (ODI), G20 governments - including those that have declared a climate emergency - continue to contribute billions of dollars in support of the fossil fuel industry.

The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) publishes an Emissions Gap Report, and an Adaptation Gap Report, comparing current practice to what is required for effective climate change mitigation and adaptation, respectively.

Popular movements

2019 also saw a tide of social and traditional media, driven by the momentum of protest movements like the Extinction Rebellion, and Fridays For Future school protests. But even this failed to generate sufficient traction at COP25. Global climate finance growth was moderate, the replenishment of the GCF was underwhelming, and carbon emissions were not slowing sufficiently. The United Nations (UN) Secretary-General, Antonio Guterres, has expressed his support for the school climate strikes, calling for the youth to lead on climate action. The Climate Emergency Fund in the USA offers finance and legal protection to climate activists in movements like the Fridays For Future school strikes and the Extinction Rebellion.

The climate protests, which have spread across developed countries and have some presence in developing countries, may be largely responsible for increased climate ambition in some countries. The raise encouraging prospects for democratic approaches to climate change - something formalised by initiatives like Ireland's citizens' assembly on climate change, the United Kingdom's citizens' assembly on climate change, and the French citizens' convention on the ecological transition.
